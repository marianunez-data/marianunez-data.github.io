Problem

Classifying Sentiment at Scale with Calibrated Confidence

Text-based platforms process thousands of user-generated documents daily. Manual sentiment classification is impractical, and uncalibrated models produce binary labels without confidence levels, making it impossible to route uncertain predictions for human review.

The challenge: build a classifier that not only predicts sentiment accurately, but produces well-calibrated probabilities enabling confidence-based routing in production.

Solution

A calibrated Logistic Regression pipeline from raw text to deployed API in 2.9 ms

Systematic comparison of 4 model families (Dummy, LR, LightGBM, DistilBERT+LoRA) with Optuna tuning, Platt calibration, and threshold optimization. The champion LR model achieves F1=0.8948 with Brier=0.0789 — enabling three routing tiers:

>85% confidence → Auto-classify
60–85% confidence → Human review
<60% confidence → Escalate

Key Findings

What the Data Revealed

Linear Separability

LR with TF-IDF bigrams outperforms DistilBERT (F1=0.8948 vs 0.8796), confirming sentiment in this corpus is linearly separable in bigram space. Complex models add cost, not accuracy.

Calibration Over Accuracy

The champion was selected for probability quality (Brier=0.0789), not just F1. McNemar's test confirms LR default and calibrated make statistically identical predictions (p=0.540), the difference is in how reliable the confidence scores are.

BERT Truncation

41.4% of reviews were truncated at max_length=256 tokens. This information loss likely limited DistilBERT's contextual advantage over bag-of-words, a tradeoff between compute cost and coverage.

Cost-Efficiency

Estimated ~$50/month (CPU) vs ~$500/month (GPU) for 1M reviews. The simpler model wins on speed (2.9ms vs 24.9ms), cost (10x cheaper), and interpretability (SHAP on every prediction). Estimates based on AWS on-demand pricing.
